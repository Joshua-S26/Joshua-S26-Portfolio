Business problem

The driving question:

“Late deliveries lower review scores and put repeat revenue at risk. Where is it happening, why, and how much revenue is exposed?”

On a marketplace, a late delivery becomes a 1- or 2-star review, and a bad first experience is what stops people from buying again. The dashboard answers that question and attaches a dollar figure to the risk so it can be prioritised.

Approach

Built as code, modelled at the right grain

The whole project is authored in Power BI Desktop developer mode, so every table, measure, and visual is a plain-text file that diffs and reviews like source code.

  1. Model the grain correctly. Two fact tables, orders (header) and order items (lines), share conformed Date and Customer dimensions. Revenue lives on the line grain; delivery and satisfaction live on the order grain.
  2. Shape the data in Power Query (M). Derive delivery_days and an is_late flag from delivered-versus-estimated dates, attach the latest review per order, and translate categories to English.
  3. Write an explicit DAX measure library. No implicit measures. Everything is named, formatted, and foldered, including the headline Revenue at Risk.
  4. Tell one decisive story on one page. A KPI strip with targets, a delivery-versus-rating scatter, a state scorecard, and a dynamic key-insight line, each earning its place.

Key insights

What the data says

Each finding is visible on the dashboard above.

4.2 → 2.6★

Delivery time predicts ratings

Average review falls from about 4.2 stars on on-time orders to about 2.6 on late ones, roughly a 1.6-star penalty. Lateness is the clearest driver of a bad review.

North / Northeast

Hot-spots are the remote states

The highest late rates sit in the northern and northeastern states furthest from the São Paulo seller base, where delivery runs about 19 to 23 days against roughly 12 nationally. High-volume Rio de Janeiro is elevated too.

$2.86M

The money at stake

Revenue tied to orders that were late or rated 2 stars or below is about $2.86M, roughly 21% of total sales. That is the repeat-purchase revenue most exposed by poor delivery.

On-time %

The fastest lever

Because lateness drives both ratings and the at-risk revenue, lifting on-time delivery toward the goal is the highest-leverage move, more than discounts or assortment changes.

Data model

A star schema with two grains

Two fact tables at different grains, sharing conformed Date and Customer dimensions. This is the strongest modeling signal, because it forces correct grain handling.

erDiagram Dim_Date ||--o{ Fact_Orders : "purchase_date" Dim_Date ||--o{ Fact_OrderItems : "purchase_date" Dim_Customer ||--o{ Fact_Orders : "customer_id" Dim_Customer ||--o{ Fact_OrderItems : "customer_id" Dim_Product ||--o{ Fact_OrderItems : "product_id" Dim_Seller ||--o{ Fact_OrderItems : "seller_id" Fact_Orders { string order_id int review_score int delivery_days bool is_late } Fact_OrderItems { decimal price decimal freight_value }

Date and Customer are conformed, so they filter both facts. Product and Seller describe order lines, so they relate to Fact_OrderItems only. There is no physical relationship between the two facts on purpose, because that would create an ambiguous filter path. The cross-grain link is made virtually in DAX, only where it is needed (see Revenue at Risk).

Key DAX

The measures that do the work

Revenue at Risk, the cross-grain headline measure

Total Sales lives on the line fact; the risk flags live on the order fact. TREATAS projects the risky order IDs onto the line grain as a virtual relationship, so revenue is summed at the correct grain with no ambiguity.

Revenue at Risk =
VAR RiskyOrders =
    CALCULATETABLE (
        VALUES ( Fact_Orders[order_id] ),
        FILTER ( Fact_Orders,
            Fact_Orders[is_late] = TRUE () || Fact_Orders[review_score] <= 2 )
    )
RETURN
    CALCULATE ( [Total Sales],
        TREATAS ( RiskyOrders, Fact_OrderItems[order_id] ) )

On-time rate and year-to-date

On-time % is measured over delivered orders so undelivered ones do not dilute it. The KPI tiles use year-to-date indicators against prior year and target.

% Late   = DIVIDE ( [Late Orders], [Delivered Orders] )
On-Time % = 1 - [% Late]

Sales YTD = TOTALYTD ( [Total Sales], Dim_Date[Date] )
Sales YoY % =
    DIVIDE ( [Total Sales] - [Sales PY], [Sales PY] )

Impact & what's next

From insight to action

Recommendation

Set delivery-time SLAs by region and prioritise seller onboarding or forward-stocking in the highest-risk northern states, plus high-volume Rio de Janeiro. Because about $2.86M (21% of revenue) is tied to late or low-rated orders, even a modest cut in the late rate protects that revenue and the repeat purchases behind it. The state scorecard makes the worklist obvious: sort by Revenue at Risk and work top down.

What I'd do next

Add a returning-customer cohort (via customer_unique_id) to turn "revenue at risk" into a measured churn number, split lateness into seller handling versus carrier transit, and promote the model to a governed Fabric medallion pipeline with scheduled refresh (see the engineering deep-dive in the repo).
